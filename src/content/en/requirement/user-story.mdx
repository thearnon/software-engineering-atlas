## What a User Story is

A User Story describes a requirement from the perspective of the person who needs the system. It names who needs something, what they want to do, and why it matters to the business.

The point is not the sentence template. The point is shared understanding of the user, goal, workflow, and acceptance criteria.

<Callout type="definition" title="Short version">
  A User Story connects a business need to scope that can be designed, tested, and delivered.
</Callout>

## Why it matters in enterprise systems

In enterprise internal tools, one requirement often touches roles, permissions, data, reports, and approval flow at the same time.

If a story only lists screens or fields, the team may deliver UI without supporting the real process.

A clear story helps developers, QA, designers, and stakeholders discuss the same workflow before solution design begins.

## Core parts of a User Story

- Actor: who uses or is affected by the feature
- Goal: the action they need to take in the workflow
- Value: the business outcome or risk reduction
- Context: the state or scenario where it happens
- Acceptance criteria: how the team knows it works
- Constraint: role, policy, data, or compliance limits

## Example case: credit limit change request

As a sales manager, I want to submit a credit limit change request so that finance can review customer risk before the new limit is applied.

That story is incomplete without acceptance criteria. The request needs a reason, may need attachments, cannot be edited after submit, and cannot be approved by its own requester.

With criteria in place, the story connects naturally to workflow, RBAC, permission matrix, database schema, and acceptance tests.

## How it connects to other topics

User Story is an entry point for many SEA topics because it turns business language into design input.

- Approval Workflow turns the story into states and transitions.
- Acceptance Testing turns criteria into test scenarios.
- Workflow Screen Design turns the task into screens and action states.
- Database Indexing may follow from frequent queries implied by the story.

## Common mistakes

- Writing developer tasks such as "build the form" without a business goal
- Forgetting negative cases such as reject, return, expired, duplicate, or no permission
- Leaving role and scope unclear, forcing RBAC decisions later
- Writing criteria too broadly for QA to verify
- Splitting stories away from the workflow they belong to

## Design checklist

- Name the real actor, not only the team requesting the work.
- Write the goal as an action in a workflow.
- State the business outcome or risk being reduced.
- Break acceptance criteria into verifiable scenarios.
- Include related role, permission, and state rules.
- Connect the story to data, reporting, notifications, or audit logs when they matter.

<Callout type="tip" title="Practical framing">
  Start with "who needs to decide what from this information" before breaking the work into screens, fields, and validation.
</Callout>
